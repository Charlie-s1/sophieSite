‘Mother Tes and the Rotten Child’ explores meal deals in relation to early adulthood, bad habits, comfort and control. I reference the mother-child dynamic that is transpired in a fraught relationship between Tesco’s and myself that is built on the mass consumption of beige mash. Horrifying suggestions of cannibalism amidst the beauty of the sugar, question who is eating who in this absurd and crashing relationship.

The materials act as a lure and a repellent. The artificial sugar window is evocative of awe-inspiring church windows with an undercurrent of manipulation that slowly rots your sweet tooth. The sandwich box refers to eye-catching advertisement and the flimsy, out of date wallpaper, that falls weak to hunger for convenience and tears through out of habit. This piece is large for a sandwich box but it is much child-sized as if you were to eat the sandwich displayed inside, you would have to infantilize yourself upon a sticky child’s chair in order to fit. The box remains shut to the inner child who wants their regurgitated lunch. Rejecting this meal deal creates an exciting and terrifying moment of realisation of, ‘You are in the World’ and that I am in control of my own life. Fly baby bird fly!!
